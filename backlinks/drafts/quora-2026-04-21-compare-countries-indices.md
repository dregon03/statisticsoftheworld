## Answer to post

The well-known ones — HDI, GDP per capita, Gini coefficient — are good starting points, but here are some less obvious indexes that are genuinely interesting:

**Less commonly cited but worth knowing:**
- **Social Progress Index** (socialprogress.org) — decouples wellbeing from income entirely. Costa Rica scores remarkably high; oil-rich Gulf states score lower than their GDP would suggest.
- **Economic Complexity Index** (Atlas of Economic Complexity, Harvard Growth Lab) — measures how sophisticated a country's export basket is. Japan and Germany score very high; resource exporters score low despite having high GDP. It's a surprisingly good predictor of future growth.
- **World Press Freedom Index** (Reporters Without Borders) — dramatic shifts year to year; useful for tracking democratic backsliding.
- **Legatum Prosperity Index** — composite of safety, governance, health, education, and social capital. Different from HDI in some interesting ways.
- **State Fragility Index** (Center for Systemic Peace) — tracks political stability risks; relevant for anyone working on geopolitics or development.

The Big Mac Index (The Economist) is famous for a reason — it's a quick-and-dirty PPP measure. Less known: The Economist also tracks a "latte index" and a "netflix index" which do similar things.

For raw macro comparisons (GDP, inflation, population, life expectancy, debt, trade) where you want to build your own comparison rather than trust someone else's weighting, [Statistics of the World](https://statisticsoftheworld.com/compare) lets you put any two countries side by side across 440+ indicators. Useful when the pre-packaged indexes don't capture what you're actually trying to understand.

The honest take: be skeptical of any single composite index. They all embed assumptions about what matters. The most interesting analyses track multiple dimensions at once and look for countries where they *diverge* — that's usually where the real story is.
