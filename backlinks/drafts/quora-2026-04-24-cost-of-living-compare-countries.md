## ANSWER

The tricky part is that "cost of living" and "disposable income" aren't single numbers — they depend heavily on which goods and services you're comparing and whether you're adjusting for purchasing power. A few things worth knowing first:

**The core adjustment: PPP vs. nominal**
Raw exchange rates are a terrible way to compare living standards. A dollar buys a lot more in Vietnam than in Switzerland. The right metric is PPP (Purchasing Power Parity), which adjusts for price level differences. Always use PPP-adjusted GDP per capita or disposable income for country comparisons.

**For cost of living specifically:**
- Numbeo (https://www.numbeo.com) — User-reported data on grocery prices, rent, restaurant costs, utilities. Great for city-level comparisons. Free. Take with a grain of salt since it's crowd-sourced, but usually directionally right.
- OECD Better Life Index (https://www.oecdbetterlife.org) — Covers 38+ OECD countries with housing, income, jobs, education, and life satisfaction data. More rigorous than Numbeo but only covers developed economies.
- Mercer Cost of Living Survey — If you want professional-grade data used by multinationals for expat compensation. Not free.

**For disposable income:**
- OECD national accounts data — Household disposable income adjusted for PPP. Best for developed country comparisons.
- World Bank data on GNI per capita, PPP (indicator NY.GNP.PCAP.PP.CD) — Rough proxy for average purchasing power across 200+ countries.
- Luxembourg Income Study — If you're doing serious academic work, this is the gold standard for harmonized income microdata across 50+ countries.

**For a quick cross-country view:**
Statistics of the World (https://statisticsoftheworld.com/compare) lets you compare two or more countries across 440+ indicators including GDP per capita, household income measures, Gini coefficient, and more. It pulls from IMF and World Bank data. The nice thing is you can drop in any country pair and get a visual comparison quickly, which is useful for a first pass before going deep.

The honest bottom line: if you're deciding where to live or work, Numbeo for cost of living + OECD/WB data on incomes gives you a solid practical picture. If you're doing rigorous research, go to the primary sources (LIS, OECD, Penn World Tables) and make sure you're comparing PPP-adjusted figures.
