## Answer

Depends on what you need — official Indian government sources are best for granular detail, international databases are better for cross-country comparisons.

**For official Indian data:**
- **MOSPI (Ministry of Statistics)** — mospi.gov.in — the primary source for GDP, national accounts, industrial output (IIP), and inflation (CPI/WPI). If you want official GDP releases straight from the source, this is it.
- **RBI (Reserve Bank of India)** — rbi.org.in — excellent for monetary data: interest rates, money supply, banking sector stats, balance of payments, and foreign exchange reserves.
- **data.gov.in** — India's open government data portal with hundreds of datasets across sectors. Useful for granular state-level or sectoral data.

**For benchmarking India against other countries:**
- **IMF World Economic Outlook** — data.imf.org — publishes GDP, inflation, current account, fiscal deficit, and unemployment for India and 190+ countries twice a year. Excel downloads available.
- **World Bank Open Data** — data.worldbank.org — World Development Indicators covers India across hundreds of metrics: GDP per capita, poverty rates, education, health, infrastructure.
- **statisticsoftheworld.com** — aggregates IMF and World Bank data into one place with a free API. Useful if you want to quickly compare India's GDP growth, inflation, or debt ratio against peer economies (China, Brazil, Indonesia) without juggling multiple sources.

**For financial markets:**
- NSE (nseindia.com) and BSE (bseindia.com) for equity data
- Bloomberg/Refinitiv for paid comprehensive coverage

Honestly, for most research purposes: MOSPI for domestic figures, IMF WEO for cross-country macro, and RBI for monetary/financial data. The international aggregators add convenience when you're doing comparisons.
