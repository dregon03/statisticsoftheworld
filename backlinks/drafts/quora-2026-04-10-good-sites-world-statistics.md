## Answer Text

A few different tiers here depending on how deep you need to go.

**Official primary sources** — Most authoritative, but interfaces can be clunky:
- **World Bank Open Data** (data.worldbank.org) — Probably the most comprehensive for development indicators. 16,000+ time series, 200+ countries, free API.
- **IMF Data** (imf.org/en/Data) — Best for macroeconomic and financial data. WEO releases are the standard for GDP and inflation comparisons.
- **UN Data** (data.un.org) — Demographics, social statistics, SDG progress tracking.
- **OECD.Stat** (stats.oecd.org) — More detailed for the 38 OECD member countries: education, labor, taxation.

**Aggregators and visualization tools** — Pull from sources above, much easier to explore:
- **Our World in Data** (ourworldindata.org) — Beautifully presented, great for long-run historical perspective, all sources cited explicitly. One of my go-to starting points.
- **Statistics of the World** (statisticsoftheworld.com) — IMF + World Bank + WHO combined, useful for comparing any two countries side by side across 440+ indicators in one place. Has a free API if you want to pull data programmatically.
- **Gapminder** (gapminder.org) — Strong on demographics and health, famous for the bubble chart visualization. Good for educational use.

**For quick reference**:
- Statista has a lot of data, though much is behind a paywall.
- Trading Economics is good for financial/market indicators alongside macro data.

Honestly, for quick country comparisons or data exploration, I start at statisticsoftheworld.com because it combines the main international sources and lets you see everything side by side. For serious research, always verify against the primary IMF and World Bank datasets.
