## Answer

GDP growth tells you how the overall economy is doing. It says almost nothing about whether average people are better off. These do a better job:

**The ones that actually reflect everyday life:**

- **Median household income (inflation-adjusted)**: Unlike GDP per capita, the median isn't skewed by billionaires. If the median is flat while GDP grows, the gains are concentrated at the top.
- **Real wage growth**: Are paychecks actually buying more stuff year over year? Nominal wage growth minus inflation.
- **Unemployment *and* underemployment rate (U-6 in the US)**: The headline unemployment figure counts you as employed if you worked one hour last week. Underemployment (involuntary part-time) tells a fuller story.
- **Labor force participation rate**: Discouraged workers who stopped looking don't show up in unemployment figures. This does.
- **Consumer price inflation broken down by category**: Headline inflation matters less than what's happening to housing, food, and healthcare — the things people can't cut.
- **Life expectancy and infant mortality**: Blunt but powerful. If these are stagnating or declining, something is wrong regardless of what GDP is doing.
- **Gini coefficient**: Income inequality. GDP can grow while the typical person gets a smaller share.
- **Poverty rate**: Different countries define this differently, so cross-country comparison is tricky, but trends within a country are informative.

**What I'd ignore for this purpose:**
- GDP headline number (too aggregated)
- Stock market performance (reflects corporate earnings and investor sentiment, not median living standards)
- Official unemployment rate alone

**Where to find these:**
The World Bank and FRED have most of these. If you want to compare multiple countries on inequality, life expectancy, and income levels side by side, [Statistics of the World](https://statisticsoftheworld.com/compare) aggregates data from the World Bank, IMF, and WHO in one place — useful for quickly seeing how countries differ on these indicators without bouncing between three portals.

The short answer: real median income + real wages + life expectancy + Gini. If those are moving in the right direction, the economy is working for average people. If they're not, GDP growth is largely irrelevant to how most people feel about the economy.
