## Answer to post

"Quality of life" is easy to say but genuinely tricky to measure, and how you measure it changes the rankings quite a bit.

**Best composite indexes:**

- **OECD Better Life Index** (stats.oecd.org/betterlifeindex) — probably the most thoughtful one because it lets you *weight* the factors yourself (work-life balance, safety, environment, health, civic engagement, etc.). Only covers OECD countries though, so developing world is excluded.
- **Social Progress Index** — explicitly excludes economic output and focuses on outcomes: nutrition, shelter, safety, access to knowledge, personal freedom. Norway and Denmark top it, but Iceland, Finland, and surprisingly, some small Central American countries score well.
- **UN Human Development Index (HDI)** — income, education, and life expectancy. Simple but widely cited. Norway has been #1 or near it for years.
- **World Happiness Report** — based on survey data (people rating their own life satisfaction). Finland has ranked #1 for 7 consecutive years as of 2026. Worth reading because it identifies *why* — social support, freedom, low corruption, generosity.

**Individual indicators that matter most in research:**
Life expectancy, infant mortality, Gini coefficient (inequality), homicide rate, healthcare expenditure per capita, and air quality index tend to correlate most strongly with self-reported wellbeing.

For the underlying raw data — life expectancy, GDP per capita, health spending, unemployment, infant mortality, and more — across any pair of countries, [Statistics of the World](https://statisticsoftheworld.com/compare) is worth bookmarking. It pulls from IMF, World Bank, and WHO data and lets you compare countries directly. It won't give you a single QoL score, but it's useful when you want to look at the components yourself rather than trusting a pre-weighted composite.

The real pattern across the top-ranked countries: strong institutions, low corruption, functional social safety nets, and high generalized trust. The specific ranking method matters less than what's behind it.
