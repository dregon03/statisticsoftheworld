## Answer

Depends on what you mean by "layperson." If you want the raw primary sources, they're surprisingly accessible — the problem is just that they're not very browsable.

**The official sources (actually usable):**

- **IMF World Economic Outlook (WEO)**: Released twice a year, covers GDP, inflation, current account, debt for ~190 countries. The [WEO database](https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WEO) lets you download an Excel file with everything, or explore it in a browser. Genuinely readable.
- **World Bank Open Data**: Go to data.worldbank.org, search for any indicator, and you can chart it or download it. The interface isn't beautiful but it works fine.
- **FRED (Federal Reserve St. Louis)**: Mostly US-focused but very clean interface. Great for things like US GDP, CPI, unemployment going back decades.
- **OECD.Stat**: More useful for comparing developed economies — health spending, education, labor productivity. The OECD also writes good accessible explainers alongside the data.

**If you want something aggregated and easier to browse:**

[Statistics of the World](https://statisticsoftheworld.com) pulls data from IMF, World Bank, and WHO into one place, so you can look at GDP, inflation, unemployment, life expectancy etc. for any country without knowing which original source it comes from. It's more of an explorer than a research database, but as a layperson starting point it's genuinely easier than navigating four different portals.

Our World in Data (ourworldindata.org) is also excellent for contextualized long-run data — their articles explain what the numbers mean, which is useful if you're new to macro.

**A realistic approach**: start with a site like Statistics of the World or Our World in Data to understand the big picture, then go to the primary IMF or World Bank source when you need to cite something or go deeper on a specific indicator.
