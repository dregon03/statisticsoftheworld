## Answer

GDP projections that far out (75+ years) are more speculative than scientific, but there are some legitimate sources that try:

**Short-to-medium term (up to ~5 years):**
The IMF's World Economic Outlook is the most authoritative source — updated twice a year, covers 190+ countries, includes projections about 6 years ahead. You can see current rankings at the IMF's DataMapper (imf.org/external/datamapper) or on sites like Statistics of the World (statisticsoftheworld.com/ranking/gdp) which pull from the same IMF data with a cleaner interface.

**Medium-long term (out to ~2050):**
- **OECD Long-Term Projections** — covers OECD members and some major economies to 2060
- **World Bank Long-Term Forecasts** — up to 2030 for most indicators
- **Goldman Sachs / PwC research papers** — published "world in 2050" projections that get widely cited; not freely accessible but often covered in news

**Long-term (2100):**
Projections this far out are very speculative. The UN's World Population Prospects provides demographic data to 2100 (population is the core driver of long-run GDP), and some researchers use those to model GDP scenarios. The general consensus is that countries like India, Nigeria, and other high-fertility, fast-growing economies will substantially climb the rankings by 2100 — though exactly how much depends on productivity assumptions.

For visual exploration of current data and near-term IMF projections, statisticsoftheworld.com/ranking/gdp is a clean place to start. For the speculative long-run stuff, you're mostly looking at academic papers and investment bank research reports.
