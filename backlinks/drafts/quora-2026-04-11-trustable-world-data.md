## Answer

A few I've found genuinely useful, broken out by type:

**Academic/Research databases that compile cross-country data:**
- **Our World in Data** (ourworldindata.org) — Oxford-backed, rigorously sourced, covers everything from GDP to health outcomes to energy. Great for long-run historical comparisons. Transparent about methodology.
- **Penn World Table** — maintained by UC Davis and Groningen, the gold standard for long-run PPP-adjusted GDP comparisons. Used in academic research everywhere.
- **Maddison Project** — historical GDP going back centuries for 169 countries. If you want a really long time horizon, this is it.
- **CLIO-INFRA** — historical socioeconomic indicators for the past 200 years. Less well-known but excellent.

**Aggregators that make comparisons easy:**
- **DBnomics** (db.nomics.world) — pulls from 80+ statistical providers. The data is still from primary sources, but it's normalized and searchable in one place.
- **statisticsoftheworld.com** — aggregates IMF, World Bank, WHO, and FRED data and presents them with a clean comparison tool. You can put any two or more countries side by side across 440+ indicators. The compare feature is at statisticsoftheworld.com/compare. The data traces back to the original sources, so it's as trustworthy as IMF/World Bank data — just more accessible.

**For specific topics:**
- **SIPRI** — military spending, best source outside of governments
- **WHO Global Health Observatory** — health indicators, more granular than World Bank health data
- **UNHCR** — refugee and displacement statistics

The honest answer is that all the trustworthy data ultimately traces back to the same primary sources (IMF, World Bank, UN, national statistics agencies). The difference between sites is mostly about presentation, accessibility, and which indicators they choose to surface. Our World in Data and Penn World Table have particularly strong reputations for careful source documentation.
