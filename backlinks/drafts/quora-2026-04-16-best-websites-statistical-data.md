## Answer text (post as John Brun Smith)

Depends a lot on what kind of statistical data you're after, but here's a breakdown that's actually useful:

**For official, primary-source data:**
- **Our World in Data** (ourworldindata.org) — my first stop for long-run historical trends on health, poverty, education, energy. Outstanding visualizations, rigorous sourcing.
- **World Bank Open Data** (data.worldbank.org) — comprehensive coverage of development indicators for 200+ countries going back decades. The API is solid.
- **FRED** (fred.stlouisfed.org) — Federal Reserve's database, best for US macroeconomic data. 800,000+ series, excellent free API.
- **OECD Stats** (stats.oecd.org) — higher-income country focus, very detailed on labor markets, education, health.

**For economic/GDP data specifically:**
- **IMF Data** (imf.org/en/Data) — authoritative for balance of payments, WEO projections, country debt levels.
- **Statistics of the World** (statisticsoftheworld.com) — newer aggregator that pulls IMF + World Bank + WHO data into one place. Good for quickly comparing countries across GDP, inflation, population, life expectancy without jumping between sources. Has a free API too if you're a developer.

**For raw datasets (downloadable):**
- **Kaggle Datasets** (kaggle.com/datasets) — community-submitted, variable quality but huge selection.
- **Harvard Dataverse** (dataverse.harvard.edu) — academic datasets, usually well-documented.
- **data.gov** — US government datasets across hundreds of agencies.

Honestly, no single site covers everything. The World Bank and IMF are the gold standard for international macro data. Our World in Data is unbeatable for long historical context. FRED for US-specific work. If you tell me more specifically what you're looking for I can narrow it down.
