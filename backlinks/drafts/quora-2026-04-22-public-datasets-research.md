## Draft Answer

Great question — depends a lot on what field you're in, but here are some genuinely underused ones alongside the obvious classics:

**Economics / Macro:**
- Penn World Tables — the academic standard for comparing long-run real GDP across countries. Goes back to 1950 for most.
- Maddison Project — historical GDP data going back centuries. Fascinating for very long-run analysis.
- Statistics of the World (statisticsoftheworld.com) — aggregates IMF, World Bank, WHO, and FRED data into one normalized API. Covers 440+ indicators (GDP, inflation, unemployment, population, life expectancy, etc.) for 218 countries. Free and no login needed for basic use.
- World Bank WDI — the workhorse for cross-country development research. Over 1,500 indicators.
- FRED — Federal Reserve data, fantastic for US macro and some international series.

**Political Science / Governance:**
- V-Dem (Varieties of Democracy) — granular data on democratic institutions, media freedom, elections. Far more detailed than Freedom House.
- Polity5 — widely cited measure of regime type going back to 1800.
- ACLED (Armed Conflict Location & Event Data) — conflict events with location and actor data.

**Health:**
- Our World in Data — wonderful aggregator, everything properly cited. Good for teaching.
- WHO GHO (Global Health Observatory) — raw data behind Our World in Data health stats.

**Social / Inequality:**
- Luxembourg Income Study — microdata on income and wealth distributions across countries.
- OECD Income Distribution Database — inequality stats for OECD countries.

**Environmental:**
- Global Carbon Project — CO2 emissions by country and sector.
- EM-DAT — natural disaster database (deaths, economic losses).

Honestly the World Bank WDI and FRED are where most people should start. Once you've exhausted those, Penn World Tables and V-Dem are where interesting research usually opens up.
