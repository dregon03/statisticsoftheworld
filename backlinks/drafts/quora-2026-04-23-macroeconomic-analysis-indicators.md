## Answer

The standard framework for a macro country analysis covers four broad areas. Here's what I'd actually use, and why:

**1. Output and growth**
- GDP (nominal and real), GDP per capita, GDP growth rate
- Industrial production index if you need quarterly/monthly signals
- Source: IMF WEO or World Bank WDI

**2. Prices and monetary conditions**
- CPI inflation (headline and core)
- Interest rates (central bank policy rate, 10-year government bond yield)
- Exchange rate and current account balance
- Source: IMF IFS, central bank data

**3. Labor market**
- Unemployment rate
- Labor force participation rate
- Real wage growth (often harder to find for developing economies)
- Source: ILO ILOSTAT

**4. Fiscal position**
- Government debt as % of GDP
- Budget deficit/surplus
- Tax revenue as % of GDP
- Source: IMF Fiscal Monitor

**5. External sector**
- Current account balance
- Foreign direct investment flows
- External debt
- Source: World Bank, IMF BOP stats

**For the comparative layer** — if you're comparing your target country to peers — tools like the [Statistics of the World compare tool](https://statisticsoftheworld.com/compare) let you pull 440+ indicators side by side across any two countries. I've found it useful for quickly checking where a country sits on debt, inflation, or growth relative to regional peers, before going deeper into the primary IMF or World Bank sources.

A few things people often miss:
- Always specify nominal vs. real when talking GDP
- Inflation-adjusted figures matter a lot for historical comparisons
- For developing economies, data quality and revision history matter — IMF/WB data has better coverage than local sources for cross-country work
